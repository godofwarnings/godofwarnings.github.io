## The first time and the second

I came across his work quite a while ago. It was one of those algorithmic accidents, a painting surfacing somewhere I wasn't looking for it (pintrest:). I registered it, appreciated it and moved on. I feel that's the thing about the internet; it has taught me to consume and continue.

Then I stumbled on it again recently (on reddit). Same paintings. But this time, something snagged. 

I sat with them longer than I meant to.

These are not dramatic paintings. Absolutely by no means. There is no mythological staging, no grand gesture. *In Bed* (1892) and *In Bed, The Kiss* (1892–93) show two figures under sheets, heads close together, eyes possibly open, possibly not. The quality of the light is morningish(...?) — that particular softness before either person has had to become themselves again. The couple looks, unmistakably, like they love each other and not in a performed way. Waking up glad the other person is still there.

Lautrec painted them from life. His subjects were the women who worked in the Montmartre brothels where he spent much of his late life — partly because he found the nightlife genuinely interesting, and partly, well you get the sense, because those women didn't recoil from him. He was one of the few men who looked at them without transaction in mind. He drew them washing, resting, laughing with each other. The tenderness in these paintings doesn't feel invented. He observed it and he put it down.

But that's what makes them hard to look at.

Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec - born in 1864 into one of the oldest aristocratic families in France, and into a condition that his family's long history of cousin marriage had helped produce. A genetic disorder meant that after he broke both legs in adolescence, they simply stopped growing. He reached a height of roughly 4'8". His torso developed normally. His face, by all accounts, was considered striking.

But the mockery was relentless. The nicknames were cruel in that specific way that takes effort — someone definitely had to sit and think those up. His father, an eccentric who found his son's appearance an embarrassment, largely withdrew. From what survived, his romantic life appears to have been almost entirely confined to paid relationships with the women he painted. He drank heavily for most of his adult life and died at 36, institutionalized, his body wrecked.

He knew all of this while he was painting *In Bed*. He knew what he was rendering and he knew, with some precision, the distance between himself and it.

## The Particular Difficulty

When people talk about artists channeling their pain and suffering into beauty, there's usually an assumption of conversion — the wound becomes the work, and somewhere in that process the artist gets something back. A muse, in the traditional sense, is that alibi. Suffering arrives with a gift attached. Pain elects you, transforms you and then hands you something in return. It makes the misery legible and even enviable. But Lautrec doesn't fit that story cleanly, because what he was painting wasn't longing sublimated into symbol.

Lautrec wasn't merely converting grief into something abstract. He was painting a specific thing: two people, warmth, mutual recognition, the sensation of being seen by someone who woke up wanting to look at you. He knew exactly what that was. The tenderness isn't metaphorical. It's rendered, specific, careful which means he had to hold the image fully in mind — what it feels like from the inside — and then put it down on cardboard with a brush, and then look at it.

There's something almost unbearable in that, if you let yourself think about it. Not the tragedy of his circumstances taken in aggregate, but this particular act: executing something beautiful you cannot inhabit. Doing it well. Maybe doing it better than anyone else could have, precisely because he was watching from outside.

I don't know whether the paintings brought him anything. I find myself hoping they did. I also suspect that the question of whether making the thing helped him is one he may have found beside the point — he made the work because he saw something true and wanted to record it. The cost of that is a separate matter which I'm not prepared enough to talk about.

## What Gets Called Depressing

The Reddit post that brought me back to these paintings the second time described them as the most depressing paintings in Western art. I understand it. It's second only to the modern habit of calling everything depressing. But I'm not sure *depressing* is quite right way to put it, or at least it's not all that's happening.

Depressing implies a closing-down. These paintings don't close down. They're genuinely warm. You can feel the weight of the figures through the sheets, the ease of a body next to a body it trusts. The difficulty isn't in the paintings themselves. It's in the parenthetical they carry once you know who made them and what his days looked like. You're holding two things at once — what is shown, and who was doing the showing — and that gap doesn't resolve. 

This is maybe closer to grief than to depression. At least grief acknowledges that something real is being mourned.

## Looking at Art That Costs Something

There's a version of art appreciation that's essentially painless — you admire the craft, you note the period, you move on. These paintings don't really allow that. Not once you know the context. And the context isn't separate from the work; it's the thing that explains why the tenderness in them feels so precise. He wasn't painting an idea of intimacy. He was painting intimacy as someone who had mostly watched it from across a room and that's why they landed differently the second time. The first time I saw them as good paintings. The second time I understood that I was looking at someone trying to get very close to something he loved, through the only means available to him.

I sat staring at them and thinking about it for a while after that. It felt like the least I could do.
